Prologue

3 NE - The morning sun of Sita spilled across the village of Mesi, gilding the polished stone and the training fields beyond. Alia’s boots thudded against the ground, sweat dripping down her temple as she swung her blade again and again. Each strike echoed faintly in the quiet compound.

Rina stood off to the side, arms crossed, her gaze unblinking. “Faster. Stronger. You have fifteen minutes to finish that sequence or we start over tomorrow.”

Alia huffed. “Fifteen minutes? Mom, it’s already been an hour!”

Rina didn’t flinch. “You’re seventeen. If you can’t keep up, you’re not ready.”

Alia’s shoulders slumped, but she glanced over at her sister. Asha crouched behind the low wall, knees tucked to her chest, eyes wide but observant. She didn’t speak, didn’t complain. She just watched.

“You know,” Alia muttered under her breath, “I could train Asha too if you let me.”

“You’re not in charge,” Rina said sharply. “You handle yourself first. That’s your job.”

Asha tilted her head, voice soft. “Alia… why do you always push yourself so hard?”

Alia gave a short laugh, a look of resignation crossed her face. “Because Mom wants perfection. And if I don’t do it, she’ll… well, she’ll start picking at you next.”

Asha stayed silent after that, but her small hand brushed against the edge of Alia’s arm. It was a quiet gesture, a reminder that she wasn’t completely alone.

Later, the three shared breakfast in the small dining room. Rina moved with precise efficiency, laying out food while issuing instructions. Alia spooned rice, her mind already racing through drills she had to finish. Asha poked at a piece of fruit, eyes darting between the two of them, absorbing the tension in every glance.

“Alia, finish your plate. Then run your drills before I check on your posture,” Rina said.

“Yes, Mom,” Alia replied, tone tight.

Asha leaned closer to her sister. “Do you… ever get tired of it?”

Alia smiled faintly, ruffling Asha’s hair. “Every day. But someone’s gotta keep us from looking like amateurs when the empire comes calling.”

Asha nodded slowly, digesting the words. She didn’t fully understand, but she admired her older sister’s strength.

Meanwhile, far beyond Sita, in the outskirts of the Surya Star System, the half-built mechaplanet Shiva lie. Sparks and welding arcs lit the skeletal frame, highlighting the immense scale of the project. Niyo adjusted her visor, leaning over the holographic projection. “We’re behind schedule,” she muttered.

Havoc stretched against a support beam. “It’s coming together. Don't worry about it.”

“You don’t get it,” Niyo snapped. “One miscalculation and everything dies.”

A faint hiss echoed through the vent above them. A thin, pale gas slithered along the floor, curling around cables and tools. Niyo waved it off. “Vent problem, probably. Keep moving.”

Havoc’s hand lingered on the railing, a flicker of unease passing over his face. He didn’t say anything.

Neither of them noticed the tiny spores floating invisibly in the mist, waiting.
