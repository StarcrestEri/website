DEFINITIONS:

Quasi-Quartz (Noun)
a rare, naturally occurring crystalline mineral composed mainly of silicon dioxide with traces of aluminum and iron oxides, found only on the planet agni. characterized by its unique lattice structure that allows it to store and release bioatomic energy efficiently. quasi-quartz is essential for advanced energy technologies and plays a crucial role in the metabolism of certain genetically enhanced individuals known as neumin. Chemical structure (SiO₂)ₓ·(AlO₃)ᵧ·(FeO)𝓏

Quasisilicatalysis (Noun)
The enzymatic-like bioatomic process by which Neumin extract energy from quasi-quartz liquid crystal solutions. This occurs within the Quasisilicaureal gland, where resonance-active receptors catalyze energy transfer from the crystal lattice without chemically breaking down the substance itself. After energy extraction, the inert fluid is excreted through sweat and urine.

Silicacrine Cycle (Noun)
The Silicacrine Cycle is a naturally occurring, monthly biological process in Neumin physiology in which the body initiates a deep systemic cleansing to remove residual quasi-quartz particles that accumulate from repeated energy extraction. Triggered by internal resonance thresholds and regulated by the Quasisilicaureal gland, this cycle involves a temporary increase in glandular activity, metabolic rate, and dermal and renal excretion. This only happens monthly in normal circumstances. Otherwise it will happen as frequently as the body needs, to avoid buildup if a neumin ingests too much Quasi-Quartz Liquid Serum (QQLS)

Quasi-Quartz Liquid Serum (QQLS, Noun)
A bioengineered or naturally derived injectable fluid composed of quasi-quartz nanocrystals suspended within a stabilized liquid crystal medium. Designed to maintain the crystalline lattice structure and bioatomic resonance properties of quasi-quartz, this serum facilitates efficient energy transfer within Neumin physiology by enabling resonance-based extraction through the Quasisilicaureal gland. Used for metabolic enhancement, the serum’s liquid crystal matrix ensures biocompatibility and prevents crystal aggregation, allowing safe circulation and optimized bioatomic energy uptake. Residual inert crystals are eliminated via natural excretory pathways and the monthly Silicacrine Cycle.

Silicacrinins (Noun)
Specialized enzymes that break down or tag inert QQR for excretion

Purge Vesicles (Noun)
Microstructures that transport QQR to excretion sites

Quasinephrines (Noun)
Hormones that regulate renal participation in the purge

Crystalline Load Index (CLI, Noun)
A metric used to determine how close a Neumin is to triggering the cycle



MEDICAL CONDITIONS:

Quasisilicatonia is a balanced or typical metabolic state that allows the body to regulate and process quasi-quartz at normal levels.

Quasisilicaneplasia is a rare genetic condition in which the subject is born without functional quasi-quartz receptors or the metabolic machinery needed to bind or utilize it.

Quasisilicadysria is a rare genetic condition in which quasi sub-par resonance, not a full defect, but unstable or inconsistent metabolization.

Quasisilicatropism is a genetic condition in which the subject is born with hyper-efficient resonance with quasi-quartz.

Quasisilicaptosis is the biological capability of Neumin to directly absorb and harness bioatomic energy emitted by quasi-quartz crystals or liquid crystal fields through resonance, without the need for ingestion or internal processing. This energy uptake occurs via specialized quasisilicaureceptors located in the skin and sensory organs, allowing ambient quasi-quartz energy to supplement metabolic reserves. While typically less efficient than active quasisilicatalysis of injected serum, Neumin exhibiting quasisilicatropism possess hyper-efficient resonance mechanisms that enable them to fully capitalize on quasisilicaptosis, rendering their passive energy absorption equally or more effective than metabolic processing.

Quasisilicacarcinergy is a hypersensitive immune response to quasi-quartz particles or resonance,  resulting in allergic reactions ranging from mild irritation to severe systemic inflammation. Individuals with quasisilicacarcinergy experience symptoms when exposed to quasi-quartz, such as rashes, respiratory distress, or neurological disturbances, due to their immune system attacking the bioatomic energy stored in or released by quasi-quartz crystals.

Quasisilicaureitis is a chronic autoimmune condition in which the body’s immune system mistakenly attacks the Quasisilicaureal gland, a specialized structure located in the ear responsible for metabolizing quasi-quartz and regulating bioatomic energy in Neumin. This immune response leads to glandular inflammation, cellular damage, and impaired metabolic function. Under normal conditions, the Quasisilicaureal gland facilitates the binding and processing of quasi-quartz crystals, a process critical for enhanced metabolic activity. However, in Quasisilicaureitis, autoantibodies and reactive T-cells infiltrate the gland, disrupting energy regulation and causing a range of symptoms. These include chronic fatigue, weakness, episodic dizziness, auditory sensitivity, fluctuating tolerance to quasi-quartz exposure (occasionally resulting in allergic-like reactions or metabolic crashes), cognitive fog (often referred to as "resonance lag"), and in some cases, mild hearing impairments due to local inflammation. The condition typically begins with subtle signs like fatigue or sensitivity to quasi-quartz but can progress to significant metabolic dysfunction and energy deficiencies if untreated. It is often misdiagnosed as Quasisilicacarcinergy, Quasisilicadysria, or Quasisilicaneplasia due to overlapping symptoms. Treatment currently centers on immunosuppressive therapies, careful regulation of quasi-quartz exposure, and lifestyle modifications to manage flare-ups, with experimental research exploring glandular regeneration through bioengineering.

Quasisilica Auto-rejection Syndrome (QARS) is a rare and severe immunometabolic disorder in which the body’s immune system misidentifies quasi-quartz, an essential energy-regulating crystalline compound, as a biological threat. This misidentification triggers a systemic rejection of both external implants and internal bioatomic energy stores. Unlike conditions that involve poor resonance or inconsistent metabolism, QARS causes a full immune response against any interface containing quasi-quartz, including augmentations, crystalline implants, and even the body's own quasi-quartz-integrated tissues. The condition often begins with localized inflammation at augmentation sites but can rapidly progress to include fevers, chronic fatigue, tissue necrosis, metabolic crashes, and seizures as immune cells destroy key components of the body’s energy systems. In advanced cases, individuals may develop bioatomic anemia, losing the ability to store or regulate internal energy and becoming dependent on synthetic, non-quartz energy systems for survival. Frequently misdiagnosed in its early stages. While there is no cure, treatment focuses on immunosuppressive therapies, surgical removal of implants, immune reprogramming, and experimental biointerface masking techniques. These options are expensive, often inaccessible, and typically reserved for military or high-status individuals. QARS is officially recognized in the Neumin Bioatomic Disorder Registry as a Class IV Immunometabolic Syndrome and is monitored under the Empire’s Quasi-Metabolic Health Directive.

Quasisilicadermal Load Syndrome (QDLS) is a localized dermatological condition in which excess quasi-quartz particles that fail to fully metabolize accumulate within the skin, forming blockages in the pores due to impaired mineral excretion. In typical Neumin physiology, trace amounts of quasi-quartz byproduct are expelled through specialized sweat glands that regulate energy waste. However, in individuals with QDLS, this process is disrupted by glandular underactivity, genetic variation, or environmental factors that inhibit dermal filtration. As a result, quasi-quartz residues begin to build up in the skin, leading to the formation of hard, often painful pustules or cyst-like lesions. These can appear as small bumps or grow into deeper, inflamed nodules that cause localized swelling, sensitivity, and in some cases, minor bleeding when ruptured. Though rarely life-threatening, QDLS is sometimes chronic and can be physically uncomfortable. Patients may experience flare-ups in response to stress, poor diet, or intense bioatomic activity, and severe cases can result in scarring or skin distortion if left untreated. Treatment typically includes topical exfoliants, dermal pore-flushing agents, and oral metabolic stabilizers that promote proper quasi-quartz breakdown. In more advanced or cystic cases, physicians may perform minor surgical extraction or apply resonance-pulse therapy to dissolve deeper deposits without damaging surrounding tissue. While manageable, QDLS remains one of the more common yet cosmetically and socially frustrating quasi-related conditions.

Quasisilicaureal Gland (QSAG) is a specialized, resonance-sensitive glandular organ located deep within the inner ear of Neumin individuals. This gland plays a central role in metabolizing quasi-quartz, a crystalline compound essential for bioatomic energy regulation. The Quasisilicaureal gland contains high-density clusters of quasisilicaureceptors, which detect and bind to quasi-quartz particles, initiating a cascade of metabolic and energetic processes. Through this interaction, the gland helps maintain stable energy resonance within the Neumin body. The gland interfaces with both the vestibular system (affecting balance and resonance sensitivity) and bioatomic circulatory pathways, allowing real-time modulation of energy uptake and release. Dysfunction or immune attack on the gland results in metabolic dysregulation, as seen in disorders such as Quasisilicaureitis, QARS, and Quasisilicadysria.


Starcrest (Prologue)

3 NE - The morning sun of Sita spilled across the village of Mesi, gilding the polished stone and the training fields beyond. Alia’s boots thudded against the ground, sweat dripping down her temple as she swung her blade again and again. Each strike echoed faintly in the quiet compound.

Rina stood off to the side, arms crossed, her gaze unblinking. “Faster. Stronger. You have fifteen minutes to finish that sequence or we start over tomorrow.”

Alia huffed. “Fifteen minutes? Mom, it’s already been an hour!”

Rina didn’t flinch. “You’re seventeen. If you can’t keep up, you’re not ready."

Alia’s shoulders slumped, but she glanced over at her sister. Asha crouched behind the low wall, knees tucked to her chest, eyes wide but observant. She didn’t speak, didn’t complain. She just watched.

“You know,” Alia muttered under her breath, “I could train Asha too if you let me.”

“You’re not in charge,” Rina said sharply. “You handle yourself first. That’s your job.”

Asha tilted her head, voice soft. “Alia… why do you always push yourself so hard?”

Alia gave a short laugh, a look of resignation crossed her face. “Because Mom wants perfection. And if I don’t do it, she’ll… well, she’ll start picking at you next.”

Asha stayed silent after that, but her small hand brushed against the edge of Alia’s arm. It was a quiet gesture, a reminder that she wasn’t completely alone.

Later, the three shared breakfast in the small dining room. Rina moved with precise efficiency, laying out food while issuing instructions. Alia spooned rice, her mind already racing through drills she had to finish. Asha poked at a piece of fruit, eyes darting between the two of them, absorbing the tension in every glance.

“Alia, finish your plate. Then run your drills before I check on your posture,” Rina said.

“Yes, Mom,” Alia replied, tone tight.

Asha leaned closer to her sister. “Do you… ever get tired of it?”

Alia smiled faintly, ruffling Asha’s hair. “Every day. But someone’s gotta keep us from looking like amateurs when the empire comes calling.”

Asha nodded slowly, digesting the words. She didn’t fully understand, but she admired her older sister’s strength.

Meanwhile, far beyond Sita, in the outskirts of the Surya Star System, the half-built mechaplanet Shiva lie. Sparks and welding arcs lit the skeletal frame, highlighting the immense scale of the project. Niyo adjusted her visor, leaning over the holographic projection. “We’re behind schedule,” she muttered.

Havoc stretched against a support beam. “It’s coming together. Don't worry about it.”

“You don’t get it,” Niyo snapped. “One miscalculation and everything dies.”

A faint hiss echoed through the vent above them. A thin, pale gas slithered along the floor, curling around cables and tools. Niyo waved it off. “Vent problem, probably. Keep moving.”

Havoc’s hand lingered on the railing, a flicker of unease passing over his face. He didn’t say anything.

Neither of them noticed the tiny spores floating invisibly in the mist, waiting.

Back on Sita, the day wound on. Alia finished her drills, muscles screaming. Rina finally nodded, satisfied. “Not bad. You’re improving.”

Alia wiped sweat from her brow, glancing at Asha. Her sister was quiet, but her eyes shone with admiration.

“You’ll be fine,” Alia said softly to Asha. “I’ll make sure of it.”

Asha smiled faintly. “I know.”

Starcrest (Main story)

6 NE - Present - Alia stepped off the teleporter platform, the tang of ionized air still clinging to her skin as the forests of Tara stretched before her. Vast, damp, and heavy with the scent of moss and alien rain. She tightened her grip on the Sudarshana, its white-blue blade humming faintly, a gift from her mother, Rina, entrusted for this very journey. The request had seemed simple: retrieve the ship and return it. Yet the undercurrents of her mother’s voice had spoken of unease, of something left unsaid. As she approached the clearing where the ship rested, its golden hull faint beneath the canopy, Alia slowed. Voices carried on the wind. One harsh, metallic through a communicator, the other cold and commanding, a woman’s tone that bristled with authority. Niyo, though Alia did not yet know her name, nor the weight it bore. The man standing in the clearing turned, his blackened armor catching the pale forest light: Havoc, commander of the Niyo Empire. His crimson visor flared as it locked onto her. “You shouldn’t be here,” he growled, slipping the communicator to his side. The forest seemed to still, the swamp beneath their feet hissing softly. “Spying on me, girl?”  

Alia’s mouth opened to protest, but Havoc wasted no time, and attacked her. The air cracked as his magic attack flared with contained energy, a strike arcing toward her chest. Alia emerged from the forest of Tara bruised but triumphant, the Sudarshana humming with the faint afterglow of combat. Havoc’s form had crumpled to the earth behind her, beaten but far from defeated. The ship’s ramp sealed as she took flight, the trees shrinking into the stormy distance. The victory was short-lived. From below, a streak of crimson lanced skyward, Havoc’s parting shot. The blast seared past her vessel, raking its hull and tearing through one of the stabilizers. Warnings flared across the console, the ship veering with an uneven hum, but it held together long enough to escape Tara’s grasp. Back on Sita, Rina was waiting—and furious. Her sharp gaze swept the scorched panels and sputtering engines.
“Do you have any idea,” she hissed, “what you’ve done to this ship?” Alia, still catching her breath, had little to answer before orders followed: “Take it to the Eclipse. Have them repair it. I’ll decide what to do with you later.”

The Eclipse, a massive cruiser adrift in Sita’s orbit, welcomed her with cold steel and the hum of industry. There, a mechanic with oil-stained gloves and a wry smile set to work, patching the wounds Havoc’s rage had left. As sparks danced from her tools, the mechanic glanced at Alia’s cutlass. “Beautiful weapon,” she said. “But it could cut deeper.” Alia hesitated only briefly before handing over the blade. Hours later, it returned to her hands, its energy core gleaming brighter than before, an edge now hotter, more powerful. The moment of reprieve shattered with a holocall from Rina. “Something’s come up,” her mother said, voice clipped. “I have to leave Sita. Park the ship. We’ll speak of your recklessness when I return. But until then, you are to watch Asha and make sure she's safe. If there's as much as a single scratch on her, you're dead.”

But Alia’s pride stung sharp. Instead of obeying, she turned her ship toward the sands of Vishnu. Logic held no sway, only the need to defy. To act. The desert planet greeted her not with silence, but with fury. A sandstorm emerged. Visibility vanished, alarms wailed, and before she could curse her decision, the ship tore into the sand, engines screaming as the ground rose to claim it. The wrecked ship lay half-buried in the rust-like sands of Vishnu, its engines silent and its hull blistered by the storm. Alia wrapped herself in a torn thermal cloak, the Sudarshana at her side, and stepped into the searing wind. The dunes stretched endless and pale beneath a burning sun. Hours passed, her water low, her temper lower, before the jagged silhouettes of a repair outpost cut through the haze. But the outpost was no sanctuary. Smoke curled skyward from the village of the Laima, a quiet desert people who dwelled beside the station. A figure in red and black armor stood at its center, cutting down resistance with brutal precision. Alia didn't think. Steel and energy clashed as she met the armored woman in the dust-choked streets, their battle crackling across the sand. At last, with a sweeping arc of her blade, Alia struck her foe down, though the woman’s body vanished in a shroud of smoke, leaving no name, no corpse, only a warning of power yet unmet.

The Laima gathered, their voices lifted in hesitant praise. They offered her water, shelter, thanks, but their hands were empty of the parts she needed.
“The Scorpionfolk hold them,” their leader explained, chitinous fingers twitching as he spoke. “We cannot pay what they demand.” So Alia went to parley with the Scorpionfolk, their barbed tails swaying as they listened. Her words, sharp as the desert wind, cut a path to compromise: leniency granted, debts delayed, and the Laima freed from their suffocating terms. Repairs came at last. The ship rose from the sands, its scars patched, its engines humming. Guilt gnawed at her resolve. She punched in coordinates for Sita, desperate to return, to avoid further wrath from Rina. But haste is its own saboteur: a slip of her fingers, a tremor of the console, and the stars wheeled wrong.

Through warped lanes of space, she fell upon a long-lost ocean world: Yamuna.

She descended onto an ancient, rusting oil rig, its spires half-swallowed by tide. From the depths, shapes stirred, merfolk, pale and wary, darting away at the sight of her. Within the rig’s decaying heart, she found a room thick with brine and the hum of dying machines. A chamber flickered weakly. Inside, a mer was imprisoned, her name etched on the console: Aila. 

With a hiss of ancient hydraulics, Alia freed her.

“Havoc,” Aila whispered, scales glistening faintly under the emergency lights. “He seeks to bring these rigs alive again… We tried to stop him. We failed.”

Her voice trembled as she spoke of the mer's plight: scattered, hunted, their waters stained by the Niyo Empire’s cruelty.

A grinding echo rolled through the rusted corridors of the oil rig. Alia stiffened, Sudarshana flaring to life in her grip. Beyond the door, the shadows clanked and hissed with the sound of heavy boots and welding torches. Havoc’s crew, already at work to resurrect the ancient machine.

She met them in the open, steel against plasma, and when the dust settled, Havoc himself lay defeated amidst the rusting decks, his plans in shambles once more.

“I’ll see this to the end,” Alia vowed, kneeling before Aila’s chamber as the mermaid’s weary eyes met hers. “Not just for your kind, for every race they’ve cast aside.”

With her ship patched together by resolve alone, she limped back to the Eclipse. The mechanic awaited her, grease-stained and grinning, and this time fitted the vessel with a life-support array, its field knitting wounds and quickening recovery.

From there, her course bent to Indra, a colossal gas giant wrapped in stormbands, its cities floating like silver lilies upon the clouds. Her arrival drew attention, none sharper than that of Luna, a faefolk whose gaze lingered on the old hull plates of Alia’s ship.

“That’s a D-class Neumin Empire craft,” Luna murmured. “Decommissioned, outlawed when Niyo took the throne. How did you- never mind. We need your help.” 

Above the city’s skyways, a mechanical wyvern screamed through the storms, rending the towers with claws of steel. Alia rose to meet it, blade cutting arcs through the lightning-charged air. From its smoking core emerged Havoc once more, fury gleaming behind his visor.

“You!” he roared, just as the wyvern ruptured in flame, casting him into the storm. His ship caught him before the abyss could, streaking away like a blade through clouds.

Indra cheered her name that night. But Alia did not linger. The trail of her journey bent next to Agni, a world of fire, rivers of magma and choking smoke. There she hunted for resources, and there she found the red-and-black armored woman, waiting among the ash.

Their blades met in a shower of sparks, heat distorting their shadows.
“You should have stayed on your homeworld,” the woman snarled through her mask. “Stay out of this, you snobby brat.” 

But Alia’s skill had sharpened; her fury burned hotter than Agni’s core. She struck, relentless, until the woman’s helmet cracked and fell away, revealing the face beneath. Rina. 

Alia speaks, her words tear from her throat like shrapnel.
“You?”

Her mother’s eyes held neither shame nor softness. Only the weight of allegiance.
“You’re making a huge mistake,” Alia spat, fury trembling in her voice. “You stand with them? With Niyo? What the FUCK is wrong with you?!"

She left her there, ash swirling in the heat, and fled to the Eclipse. Once again, the mechanic awaited.

The Eclipse trembled as sirens split its steel corridors. From the void beyond, a familiar signature bled across the scanners. Rina’s ship, cannons blazing.

“Alia!” her voice thundered over the comms, sharp as a blade drawn too long. “You will come with me.”

The dogfight tore through the upper orbit, mother against daughter, sparks of antimatter streaking the darkness. Hulls screeched, shields failed, and both ships spiraled into the cold abyss below: the frozen wasteland of Himavat.

They stumbled from the wreckage, breath like ghosts, the snow swallowing their shouts. The argument that followed cut deeper than any blade.
“You think this is about loyalty?” Rina spat, the storm clawing at her cloak. “You were never the one I cared for. Not like Asha, not like Sami.”

The words carved hollow wounds in the howling wind. Before Alia could answer, Rina turned and vanished into the blizzard, her shadow torn away by the storm.

Alia searched until her limbs ached, until frost kissed her lashes. But the storm took more than warmth; it stole her path. In its wake, she found an unlikely light: a village buried beneath the ice, home to the ‘undesirables’ cast aside by the Niyo Empire.

There she met a girl pale as the moonlight, her fangs catching the lantern’s glow, a vampire, exiled and hunted. In that bleak cold, they found warmth in each other’s presence, something soft among the iron of war.

The villagers, hardened by exile yet generous in their defiance, restored Alia’s battered ship. And so she rose from the snows, the promise of vengeance and justice burning like a second sun in her chest. Her course: the heart of the Niyo Empire.

Through the gates of Shiva’s mechaplanet she cut, until at last the throne’s shadow loomed. But the path to Niyo’s chambers was barred. Havoc and Rina, side by side, their weapons drawn.

The duel was fierce, fury against fury, past against present. At last, Alia stood over them, breath ragged, blade gleaming. She entered the chamber.

Niyo turned, a slow smile curling at the edges of her lips as the shell of Shiva’s black hole core yawned open behind her. The machinery shrieked. The stars bent. Gravity became a predator. She would open and close the core, manipulating gravity at her whim. However, after she gets defeated, she refuses to let Alia live. Her body convulsed. She looked up at Alia, her eyes looked desperate. "Alia... H-help me...", it was the true Niyo. The true Niyo begged Alia to help her, before a parasitic arm bursts out of her side. Then another. Her entire body bursts, the only thing standing in her place is the parasitic Vortex Queen. After Alia eventually defeats the vortex queen, the chamber's black hole core became destabilized. It opened and remained opened for quite a while. The pull seized everything. Loose steel, fallen banners, the empress herself. The vortex queen's scream was lost to the void as she was dragged into the collapsing core. Alia reached for the controls, for anything, but the tide was too strong. Her blade slipped from her fingers as the void claimed her too.

36 NE - Alia awoke to birdsong. Real birds, fluttering through shafts of pale light. The air was warm. Greenery crawled over the steel bones of Shiva, its once-black areas softened by parks and plazas. Where there had been ash, there was now a city breathing. She stumbled from the forgotten chamber where the singularity had spat her out. It didn’t welcome her back. Guards spotted her on the palace outskirts, accused her of trespassing, and dragged her in chains to a sterile cell. Hours blurred. Then a little girl, about 11 years old, walks up to Alia's cell, grinning ear to ear. "Hey! You look kinda familiar! What's your name?"

“Asha…?” Alia’s voice trembled. "Do you not know who I am?"

The little girl tilted her head. "Hey, that's my mama's name! Do you know her?"

Alia, still distorted, tilted her head. "No, that's not right, just forget it. Asha's around your age, so there's no way in hell she'd be your mother. Sorry, kiddo."

After a long while of waiting, a summons finally came. The guards take Alia into a throne room and force her to kneel before the queen. The queen looks down at Alia, her eyes narrowing a bit, before she lets out a small snicker.

"Hey there, stranger. It's been a while." She said. The woman who spoke to Alia was very familiar looking. She was older than memory but younger than the throne demanded. 41, her face marked not by wrinkles but by the weight of command. Her eyes softened. “It’s really you, Alia. Am I right?” she asked.

Alia frowned a bit, trying to think. "Sorry, how do we know each other? Jesus, sorry this is all so bizarre. I was JUST fighting Niyo and- where am I? What is this place?"

The queen frowned in further confusion. "So you're not Alia?"

Alia quickly stammered. "No, no, I'm Alia but- I'm so lost right now. Sorry this is all so confusing and- I just don't know what's going on or where I am. It's all so overwhelming, I'm not in any condition to think complex thoughts right now, I just-"

"Alia," The queen interrupted, "You're fine. Just take a deep breath, and start from the beginning. You mentioned Niyo. Start there."

Alia took a deep breath and told the queen about the entire battle between her and Niyo, and how she was pulled into the black hole core of Shiva almost immediately after the fight ended. "So that's pretty much where I'm at now."

"Alia." The queen started to rub her temples. "That all happened 30 years ago. Everyone thought you died after that fight."

"What do you mean that all happened 30 years ago?! Clearly it JUST happened!" Alia's voice raised and she became more visibly distressed. "I- I need to go find my mother and- and Asha and- and-"

"Alia. Alia. Calm down. Relax. Take a deep breath. Just listen to me. It's all okay. You're fine." The queen started to console her.

"Who even are you? You're the queen yeah but like what's even your name? Surely you don't just go by 'The Queen'" Alia asked, her voice becoming a bit less desperate.

"I'm Queen Asha of the Surya Star System. And this here," She gestured to the girl Alia had spoken to a while ago, who was now standing next to Alia. "Is my daughter, Ana'a."

Alia tried to process everything. She looked over at Ana'a, then at Asha. She stared for a few moments before breaking down into tears. "No, no, no, this can't be happening. It's... It's all a lie, isn't it? This is just a dream. I was there, it- you... you were 11... no, it isn't you, this isn't you. You're just 11, you're playing games with me. Stop it. Stop it... No, you're not Asha, you aren't. I'm going to find Asha and... and mother, and..."

As Alia began to hyperventilate, Asha silently ordered her guards to sedate her. Once Alia came to, she was lying on a giant bed in an enormous room littered with gold plating and beautiful art pieces. For weeks, she paced around the room, refusing to eat unless she was alone, had an unstable sleep schedule, and she would frequently try to break out of the room to find the "real Asha". Eventually, Asha had enough and sent a therapist into the room once a week to talk sense into Alia. After a while, Alia started to learn grounding skills from the therapist and began showing improvement. The following month, she saw Asha sitting at the edge of the bed. She sat up and groaned. "Alright, alright, I'm okay now. Please, just fill me in on everything. Clearly some time fuckery happened with that black hole. I can accept that. But please, fill me in on everything that happened in the two seconds that I was gone."

Asha explained it all: their mother’s lies, her hollow reign, the quiet passing of a crown unto unwilling shoulders. “I never wanted this,” Asha confessed. “But someone had to prevent another tyrant.” The galaxy was at war again. The Vortex Queen’s death had scattered spores that tainted whole star systems, and Sol Star System’s forces blamed Surya. Bloodshed loomed on every border.

They walked the length of the upper terrace, past gardens grown over steel and glass, past courtyards that smelled faintly of rain and ash. Alia found herself speaking more than she expected, recounting fragments of her years before the black hole, the faces she remembered, the streets that had long since been paved over. Asha listened with a quiet attentiveness, sometimes amused, sometimes somber, filling in the gaps with how things had changed. The canals had been rebuilt, the trade lines redrawn, the old defense grids stripped down and reforged. People had left, others had arrived, and the Surya System had learned to stand on fractured ground.

It felt like catching up with a world she was no longer truly part of. Every so often, Alia asked about the people she used to know. Old commanders and merchants. Some were gone, some forgotten, some had clawed their way into new positions of power. There was a quiet melancholy to Asha’s voice when she spoke of them, a tone born from years of difficult choices.

Only near the end of their walk did Alia stop. Her eyes lingered on a faded mural stretched along the courtyard wall. Among the painted silhouettes stood a figure she could never mistake. Her mother, Rina, depicted in pale royal colors. The sight pressed heavily against her chest.

“She was part of this,” Alia said quietly.

Asha hesitated, her gaze shifting from the mural to Alia. “Not in the way you want to believe,” she replied, her voice firm but not unkind.

Alia turned her eyes back to the wall. Questions stirred in her, questions that had gnawed at her since the moment she returned. Had Rina missed her? Had she grieved? Or had she simply lived on without her?

Asha sensed the turn in her thoughts. She spoke quickly of other matters, of fleets gathering at the borders and the unease creeping through the system. They left the mural behind, yet Alia felt its weight follow her long after.

That night, when the corridors were silent and the palace lights dimmed, she could not let it rest. The image of Rina’s calm face in that painting haunted her. She needed to know. She needed to see for herself the truth that Asha had not said aloud. The library would have answers, if she was willing to look.

The capitol library was silent except for the soft hum of archival servers. Alia sat alone in the glow of her holopad, scrolling through decades of preserved footage. One after another, the records unfolded: celebrations, reforms, ceremonies. Rina’s face filled the screen again and again, not grieving, not hollow, but composed. Thriving. Smiling beside new allies. Holding her other daughters close. Her empire had flourished. Her life had gone on. Alia’s breath grew shallow as she searched, skipping ahead, rewinding, desperate for a sign: a quiet moment of sorrow, a whispered name, a flicker of regret. She found none. Then, a recording from the weeks after the black hole incident played. Rina stood before the court, her voice steady, her gaze unwavering. There was no mourning in her eyes. Only calculation, only the weight of a crown she had already chosen to wear.

The holopad slid from Alia’s fingers and hit the marble floor with a sharp crack. Footsteps behind her. “Alia…” Asha’s voice was low.

Alia turned slowly, her eyes burning red. “You knew.”

Asha hesitated. “You were not supposed to see those.”

Something inside Alia snapped. She shoved past her sister, storming into the corridor. “Don't say that. Don't you dare.”

“Alia, wait!” Asha hurried after her.

Alia stopped and spun, tears streaking down her face. “Wait for what? For her to come back and suddenly decide she cared? I spent my life trying to be worth her love. I wanted a mother who would miss me, who would grieve me. And she didn't. Not when I was here, and not when she thought I was dead.”

Asha reached for her, arms wrapping around her shoulders. Alia resisted, trembling, then broke. The tears came in violent, shuddering sobs. “I was not enough,” she whispered. “She lived her life without me. She was happy. And I was just gone.”

“I know,” Asha murmured, her voice breaking. “But she's dead now. That chapter is over.”

Alia’s grip tightened, her voice rising with a bitter edge. “How could she just live with it? Rule, smile, build a new federation, and never once wish I was in it? How could she do that?”

Asha held her tighter, saying nothing more, because there was no answer that would make it less true. She doesn't cry, her face conflicted with having her sister at her side for the first time in 30 years.

For a long while, they stood in the quiet of the courtyard, the wind tugging softly at their clothes. Eventually, the distant hum of the city reminded them both of the world still turning, of duties neither could escape. Asha guided Alia back toward the palace, her hand never fully leaving her sister’s arm, as if letting go might cause her to vanish again. 

The next days passed in a blur of hushed voices and closed doors. Physicians examined her, advisors whispered in corners, and everywhere Alia walked, soldiers watched. At last, Asha brought her to a chamber near the heart of the palace, its walls gleaming with marble and glass, and told her it would be hers for now.

“It’s to protect you,” she insisted. “Let me shield you, just this once.” Alia pressed her palms to the cold window. After Asha left, Alia sat there in silence, before running after her.

“You sound like her,” Alia spat when she confronted Asha in the hall. “You sound like Rina.”

Asha’s glare cut the air. “I am not our mother. But I am your queen. And you know what, I don't care if you think rules are boring or dull. To keep you from danger, I am the law.”

With the wave of her hand, her guards shove Alia back into the room. The door closed again. The lock clicked. Alia sat for hours, bored and anxious. She needed to escape. She looked over at the window, and an idea popped into her head. She attempted to open it, but it wouldn't budge. She then started hitting it. When the window finally shattered under her fist, alarms screamed across Shiva. Troops swarmed the district. Streets closed. Drones swept the skies. Through them all, Alia ran. Toward the shipyards, toward her past. Her old D-class vessel waited beneath a tarp of dust and disuse. Its hull creaked, lights flickered like ghosts. It roared to life, then died, strangled by a remote override. Asha was already there, flanked by her agents.

“Alia,” she said, breath trembling just beneath the surface. “Get out of there.”

Alia climbed from the cockpit, boots clanging on the cold metal. “I have to go, Asha. There’s a war out there—”

“You cannot do this,” Asha shouted across the platform, her voice breaking. “You cannot throw yourself into this war. I spent thirty years mourning you! I finally came to terms that you were gone! I can't lose you again!”

Alia stepped down from the hatch, trembling. “If there’s war, I fight for you. For my little sister.”

Asha laughed. A bitter, hollow sound. “I am not your little sister anymore. I was eleven when you disappeared,” Asha continued, her voice trembling but calm. “Now I am forty-one. I’ve carried this system, I’ve buried the people who tried to own it, I’ve made choices you’ve never had to make. You’re twenty, Alia. Twenty. You came back untouched while the years ground the rest of us down. You are just a kid.” She turned, gripping her own arm as if to stop herself from trembling. Her breath hitched, just once. “I can’t lose you again. Not like this. Not to the war.”

Alia stepped forward slowly, placed her forehead to Asha’s shoulder, and whispered, “Then trust me to survive it.”

Silence stretched. The hangar’s alarms were still screaming. The soldiers waited for an order. Asha’s hand hovered in the air, curled into a fist, then slowly loosened. She wiped her eyes with her sleeve, leaving a smear of tears on royal silk. When she finally spoke, her voice was barely a thread.

“You will send word. Always. If you vanish, I will come for you.” Asha murmured. “Do not make me grieve twice.”

Alia nodded, a small, guilty smile tugging at her lips. “Deal.”

The queen stepped back, and with a motion, hesitant and reluctant, the clamps on the D-class ship released. As the ship’s engines roared to life, Asha stood rooted, her face expression unreadable. Only when the hull became a distant ember did her composure fully break. She turned from the hangar, eyes red, and whispered to herself, “Don’t make me regret this.”

After Alia leaves, Asha wipes her tears and turns to her advisor, "Make sure to keep tabs on that ship. I had a tracker planted on it. If it leaves towards the Sol Star System, notify me immediately. She is not to go anywhere near that system. She is fine to participate in the battles in Surya, but not Sol."

Agni, the first planet in orbit, is renowned for its abundance of rare crystals and ores, most notably Quasi-Quartz, which is found nowhere else in the galaxy. It is home to three dominant races: the formidable Cheros, the secretive and calculating Deimos, and the industrious Pumilos, known for their masterful blacksmithing and mining skills. While the Cheros and Pumilos remain largely independent, they willingly supply the Niyo Empire with the resources it demands, driven by pragmatism rather than loyalty.
Vishnu, the second planet from the star, is a harsh desert world known for its vibrant dyes and rich mineral exports. Its arid landscapes are dominated by mesas, dunes, and cacti. The planet is home to two major races: the resourceful Laima and the warlike Tlincallis. Though they share the same world, the two have long been rivals, competing fiercely over territory and dwindling resources. After only a few months under Niyo’s rule, the Tlincallis seized control of the majority of Vishnu’s supply chains, rendering the Laima helpless against the planet’s worst drought in nearly 500 years. 
The third planet, Sita, and its moon, Tara, form a temperate pair within the system. Sita, with its oceans and grasslands, is the homeland of the Neumin, the dominant race of the empire. Tara, its lush, tropical moon, is home to the Kero, a group of semiaquatic people adapted to life in its dense forests and murky swamps. Though different in terrain, both worlds are rich in biodiversity and have long stood as cultural and strategic centers in the Surya System.
Yamuna, an ocean-covered planet in the asteroid belt of the Surya System, was only rediscovered when Alia accidentally input the wrong warp coordinates while attempting to return to Sita. Shrouded in mystery, Yamuna remains one of the least understood worlds in the system. Its endless seas are ridden with rusted, decaying oil rigs, remnants of a long, forgotten era, the reason for its long abandonment remains unknown. It is the ancestral home of the Mers, an aquatic people who now live in hiding, driven into the depths by imperial expansion and fear. 
Indra, the massive gas giant of the Surya Star System, serves as its central trade hub and cultural crossroads. Suspended within its pink and white clouds are vast floating cities, each powered by ancient Neumin tech and modern adaptations of bioatomic energy harnessed from quasi-quartz cores. As the most urbanized world in the system, Indra buzzes with commerce, diplomacy, and quiet tensions. A melting pot of language and culture, Indra’s floating markets and skyports are filled with traders peddling rare minerals from Agni, biomechanical artifacts from Sita, and salvaged weaponry from the pre-Niyo era. Above all, Indra remains a symbol of both what was lost when the old Neumin ideals fell, and what still endures in the shadows of empire.
Himavat, the final planet in orbit, is a subzero world almost entirely encased in ice. Once a lush, tropical planet, it was violently knocked out of its original orbit by an unknown planetoid in 55 BNE, triggering a catastrophic climate shift. By 50 BNE, Himavat had completely frozen over, forcing the native Kero to abandon their ancestral home and resettle on Tara, where they now thrive. In 2 NE, the Niyo Empire began relocating so-called “undesirables” from Sita to Himavat, condemning them to harsh labor in brutal conditions under the guise of earning freedom. The planet now stands as both a frozen graveyard of lost ecosystems and a symbol of imperial cruelty. 
What began as an honest effort to harness the power of a black hole took a dark turn. The scientist Niyo, once a brilliant mind, was driven mad by the black hole’s radiation. Her original design, a massive energy-harvesting ring meant to power the entire star system, was abandoned. In its place, she constructed a vast shell around the black hole, transforming it into a planetary superweapon. No longer content with aiding Neuminity, Niyo now sought to rule it.
